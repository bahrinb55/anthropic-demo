Historical Episodes

2008 Global Financial Crisis
Jan 2008 – Dec 2009
Avg Fed Rate: 1.04%
Avg Inflation: 1.76%
Peak Inflation: 5.50% (Jul 2008)
The Fed slashed rates from 5.25% to 0.05% in 14 months — the fastest easing cycle in modern history. Inflation briefly spiked to 5.5% on oil prices, then cratered to -2% as demand collapsed. The rate cuts were reactive, not preventive.
Post-GFC Zero Rate Era (ZIRP)
Jan 2010 – Nov 2015
Avg Fed Rate: 0.12%
Avg Inflation: 1.68%
Peak Inflation: 3.81% (Sep 2011)
Five years of near-zero rates. Despite unprecedented monetary stimulus, inflation stayed below the 2% target for most of this period — proof that low rates alone don't guarantee inflation. Structural headwinds (deleveraging, low growth expectations) dominated.
Gradual Normalization Cycle
Dec 2015 – Jul 2019
Avg Fed Rate: 1.47%
Avg Inflation: 2.00%
Peak Inflation: 2.95%
The Fed raised rates 225 bps over 3 years. Inflation stayed anchored near 2%. This is the textbook "successful soft landing" — rates rose alongside a growing economy without triggering a disinflationary shock or recession.
COVID-19 Shock & Stimulus Flood
Mar 2020 – May 2021
Avg Fed Rate: 0.09%
Avg Inflation: 1.01%
Peak Inflation: 4.93% (May 2021)
Emergency cuts to 0.05% + $4T in fiscal stimulus flooded the economy. Inflation was initially suppressed by the lockdown demand shock, then began surging rapidly from mid-2021 as supply chains broke and demand roared back. The Fed called it "transitory" — a characterization that would prove deeply costly.
Post-COVID Inflation Surge
Jun 2021 – Jun 2022
Avg Fed Rate: 0.14%
Avg Inflation: 6.67%
Peak Inflation: 8.98% (Jun 2022)
The worst policy error of the era. The Fed held rates at ~0.08% while inflation raced from 5% to 9% — the highest since 1981. Real rates turned deeply negative (-8%). Russia's invasion of Ukraine in Feb 2022 poured fuel on the fire via energy & food prices. The Fed fell 12–18 months behind the curve.
Aggressive Tightening Cycle (Volcker-Style)
Mar 2022 – Jul 2023
Avg Fed Rate: 3.65%
Avg Inflation: 5.92%
Total Hikes: +525 bps in 16 months
The Fed hiked 11 consecutive times, including four 75 bp "jumbo" hikes — the most aggressive tightening since Paul Volcker in 1980. Inflation peaked in June 2022 and began falling steadily, validating the 18–24 month transmission lag. The economy absorbed the shock without a formal recession (the famed "soft landing").
Soft Landing & Normalization
Aug 2023 – Present (2026)
Avg Fed Rate: 4.85%
Avg Inflation: 2.74%
Trend: Disinflation ongoing
Inflation has declined from 9% to ~2.4% while unemployment remained near historic lows. The Fed began cutting in Sep 2024. As of early 2026, rates are at 3.64% and inflation is approaching — though not yet firmly at — the 2% target. This remains one of the most successful disinflation episodes in Fed history.

Key Economic Relationships

⌛

The Long & Variable Lag

Milton Friedman's famous observation holds: monetary policy affects inflation with a delay of 18–24 months. Rate hikes today suppress inflation the year after next — making real-time policy calibration extremely difficult.

📈

Contemporaneous Correlation is Misleading

The raw r = +0.30 suggests rates and inflation move together — but this is a spurious / reverse-causation signal. The Fed raises rates in response to high inflation. Only the lagged analysis reveals the true disinflationary effect.

🔒

Zero Rates Don't Guarantee Inflation

2010–2015: rates at 0.05–0.25% for 5 years, yet inflation averaged only 1.68%. Monetary stimulus is necessary but not sufficient — fiscal policy, credit demand, and supply-side factors all play critical roles.

⚡

Supply Shocks Override Monetary Policy

Both the 2008 oil spike and 2022 energy/food surge drove inflation well above the Fed's control. Cost-push inflation from supply disruptions responds poorly to rate hikes — hiking into a supply shock risks crushing demand without fixing the root cause.

🎯

The 2% Target as Anchor

The Fed's 2% inflation target acts as a credibility anchor. When well-anchored (2010–19), even low rates kept inflation near target. When credibility eroded (2021–22 "transitory" narrative), expectations de-anchored and inflation spiraled well above 2%.

⚖

Speed of Hikes Matters

The 2015–18 cycle (gradual: +25bp every quarter) kept inflation stable. The 2022–23 cycle (aggressive: +75bp repeatedly) caused a sharper but faster disinflation. Velocity of tightening determines both the speed of impact and collateral damage to financial markets.

Methodology: CPI inflation is computed as year-over-year % change in CPIAUCSL (Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers, Not Seasonally Adjusted). Federal Funds Rate is FEDFUNDS (Effective Federal Funds Rate, monthly average). Data spans January 2007 – January 2026 (228 overlapping monthly observations after YoY differencing). Lagged correlations measure Pearson r between Fed Funds Rate at time t and CPI inflation at time t+N. All data sourced from the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis (FRED) API.
