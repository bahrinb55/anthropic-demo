Analysis & Conclusions

Phillips Curve Basics The Phillips Curve (A.W. Phillips, 1958) posits an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation: low unemployment leads to higher wages and prices. Expected Pearson r is negative.

Full Period 2005-2025 Across all 251 months the correlation is weak, largely because COVID-19 (Apr 2020: unemployment 14.8%) and the supply-chain inflation surge (CPI peaked 9.0% in Jun 2022 while unemployment was below 4%) introduced positive co-movement that dominates the simple correlation.

Pre-COVID (2005-2019) The negative relationship re-emerges: unemployment fell from ~5% to 3.5% while inflation stayed anchored near 2%, consistent with a flat negatively-sloped Phillips curve. Fed credibility anchored expectations (the "missing inflation" puzzle of the 2010s).

COVID & Supply Shocks (2020-2025) The pandemic breaks the framework. Unemployment spiked to 14.8% with only mild deflation, then recovered while inflation exploded due to supply chains, energy shocks, and fiscal stimulus. This period shows a positive correlation.

Verdict The Phillips Curve does not hold robustly as a simple bivariate relationship over the past 20 years. Modern central-bank models augment it with inflation expectations, import prices, supply shocks, and non-linear dynamics. The 2023-2024 "soft landing" further illustrates the relationship's instability.
