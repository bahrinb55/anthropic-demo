Reading the Timeline

The chart above tells the story of two major economic crises and the Fed's response to each. During the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the Fed slashed rates from 5.25% to near-zero in just 15 months as inflation collapsed. Then during the COVID-19 pandemic, rates were again cut to zero — but this time inflation surged to 9%, forcing the most aggressive tightening cycle since the 1980s.

Notice the key pattern: interest rates always follow inflation with a lag. The Fed is fundamentally reactive — it raises rates after inflation has already risen, and cuts after the economy has already weakened.

Key Finding: Inflation Leads, the Fed Follows

The contemporaneous correlation is moderate (r = 0.31) — the two series move in the same direction, but not tightly. This makes sense: the Fed raises rates because inflation is high, creating a positive association.

The strongest correlation is r = 0.71 at lag -18 months, meaning inflation changes lead Fed Funds Rate changes by approximately 18 months. This confirms the Fed is primarily reactive — it adjusts policy after observing sustained inflation trends.

As the lag becomes positive (Fed leads inflation), the correlation turns negative around +15 to +24 months. This is the transmission mechanism at work: rate hikes today reduce inflation 12–18 months in the future.

3. The Real Interest Rate: The True Policy Stance

Real rate = Fed Funds Rate minus CPI inflation. Positive = restrictive. Negative = accommodative.

Real Interest Rate Over Time

Green bars = positive real rate (tight policy). Red bars = negative real rate (loose policy). The deeper the red, the more stimulative conditions are.

Why the Real Rate Matters More Than the Nominal Rate

The nominal Fed Funds Rate alone doesn't tell you whether policy is tight or loose. A 5% rate with 3% inflation is restrictive (real rate = +2%). But a 5% rate with 9% inflation is actually highly accommodative (real rate = -4%).

Key observations from the real rate chart:

2009–2015: Mild negative real rates. With rates at zero and inflation at 1–2%, real rates were only slightly negative. This "slow recovery" period shows the limits of zero-rate policy when inflation is already low.

2021–2022: Deeply negative real rates (-8.9% at worst). The Fed kept rates at zero while inflation surged to 9%. This was the most accommodative monetary policy in modern history — and it shows why inflation spiraled. Borrowing was essentially free in real terms, fueling demand.

2023–2025: Positive real rates returned. With rates at 5.33% and inflation falling below 3%, real rates became meaningfully positive (+2 to +3%), finally applying genuine restrictive pressure. This is what ultimately cooled inflation.

4. The Transmission Mechanism: Do Rate Hikes Actually Work?

Measuring how a 12-month change in the Fed Funds Rate affects future inflation at different horizons.

Rate Change Impact on Future Inflation

Correlation between 12-month rate change and future inflation change at 6, 9, 12, 15, and 18-month horizons.

The Answer: Yes, But With a Long Delay

The chart above shows the correlation between a trailing 12-month change in the Fed Funds Rate and the subsequent change in inflation at various horizons:

At 6 months: r = -0.10 — almost no effect. Rate hikes haven't had time to propagate.
At 12 months: r = -0.24 — the effect is emerging. Inflation begins to respond.
At 18 months: r = -0.41 — meaningful negative correlation. Rate hikes are clearly reducing inflation.

This confirms the textbook "long and variable lags" described by Milton Friedman. The Fed must act well in advance of desired inflation outcomes, which is why preemptive action beats reactive policy. The 2021–2022 experience — waiting too long to hike — made the required tightening far more aggressive.

6. Key Economic Insights

The most important lessons from 20 years of data.

18 mo

The Monetary Policy Lag

Rate changes take 12–18 months to fully flow through to inflation. The Fed hiked aggressively starting March 2022, but CPI didn't peak until June 2022 and didn't fall below 4% until mid-2023 — exactly 12–15 months later.

-8.9%

2021: Behind the Curve

The Fed kept rates at zero as CPI surged from 1.4% to 7.2% (Jan–Dec 2021). The real rate hit -8.9% — the most accommodative stance ever. This delay forced far more aggressive hikes later.

0% → 5.33%

Fastest Tightening Since 1980s

From March 2022 to August 2023, the Fed raised rates by 525 basis points in just 18 months — the steepest hiking cycle in over four decades, to combat 40-year-high inflation.

9% → 2.4%

Soft Landing Achieved

Inflation fell from 9% (Jun 2022) to ~2.4% (Jan 2026) without a recession — a historically rare outcome. The Fed managed to cool price pressures through sustained positive real rates.

Asymmetric

Cuts Fast, Hikes Slow

The Fed cuts quickly in crises (GFC: 5.25%→0% in 15mo; COVID: instant to zero) but hikes gradually. This asymmetry creates a structural bias toward accommodative conditions.

2%

The Elusive Target

The Fed's 2% inflation target was rarely hit precisely. CPI spent 2012–2020 mostly below target, then dramatically overshot in 2021–2022. By early 2026, inflation is finally near the target zone.

7. How the Mechanism Works: The Causal Chain

From Rate Decision to Price Change

The relationship between the Federal Funds Rate and CPI inflation is not direct — it works through a multi-step transmission chain:

Step 1: The Fed sets the overnight rate — the rate banks charge each other for overnight loans. This is the starting point of all interest rates in the economy.

Step 2: Market rates respond — mortgage rates, car loan rates, corporate borrowing costs, and credit card rates all adjust. Higher Fed rates → higher borrowing costs throughout the economy.

Step 3: Spending and investment slow — when it costs more to borrow, consumers spend less on homes and big-ticket items, and businesses invest less. Demand drops.

Step 4: Inflation responds — with less demand chasing goods and services, price pressures ease. This is the final stage, and it takes 12–18 months to fully materialize.

The 2022–2024 case study: The Fed started hiking in March 2022 (Step 1). Mortgage rates doubled to 7%+ by late 2022 (Step 2). Housing and durable goods spending slowed through 2023 (Step 3). CPI inflation fell from 9% to 3% over 2022–2024 (Step 4). Total time: ~24 months from first hike to inflation nearing target.

Why the Correlation Isn't Perfect

Several factors weaken the statistical relationship between rates and inflation:

Supply shocks. The 2021–2022 inflation surge was partly driven by supply chain disruptions and energy prices — factors outside the Fed's control. Rate hikes cool demand-driven inflation but can't fix supply problems.

Expectations. If markets believe the Fed will fight inflation, long-term inflation expectations stay anchored, reducing the amount of tightening needed. This "credibility dividend" explains why 2022's hikes worked faster than many predicted.

Zero Lower Bound (ZLB). During 2009–2015 and 2020–2021, rates at zero couldn't go lower. Different inflation environments (deflation risk vs. surging prices) with the same nominal rate distort the correlation.

Fiscal policy. The massive COVID fiscal stimulus (trillions in direct payments) overwhelmed the Fed's accommodative stance, contributing to inflation that monetary policy alone couldn't have anticipated or prevented in real-time.

9. Conclusion

The Relationship in One Sentence

The Federal Funds Rate and CPI inflation are positively correlated contemporaneously (r = 0.31) because the Fed raises rates in response to inflation, but rate hikes negatively impact future inflation (r = -0.41 at 18 months) through the monetary transmission mechanism.

Six Takeaways from 20 Years of Data

1. The Fed is reactive. Cross-correlation peaks at -18 months (r = 0.71). The Fed responds to inflation — it rarely preempts it.

2. Rate hikes work, with a lag. The transmission mechanism takes 12–18 months but reliably reduces inflation. The 2022–2024 cycle confirmed this.

3. Real rates matter more than nominal rates. The deeply negative real rates of 2021 (-8.9%) were far more stimulative than zero nominal rates in 2010 (+0.1% real). Policy stance must be measured in real terms.

4. The zero lower bound is a real constraint. When rates can't go lower, the Fed loses its primary tool — explaining the slow post-2008 recovery vs. the inflationary explosion of 2021.

5. Speed of response matters. The delayed 2021 response (holding at zero as inflation surged to 7%) necessitated the most aggressive tightening in 40 years. Earlier, smaller hikes could have prevented the worst.

6. Soft landings are possible. The 2022–2026 episode demonstrates that aggressive but well-communicated tightening can reduce inflation from 9% to 2.4% without recession — the Fed's best outcome in decades.
